Understanding the Threat Myth: The Real Roots of Anti-Immigrant Fear

The “Threat Myth” refers to the false and typically ideologically driven belief that immigrants endanger a nation’s stability, cultural identity, or safety. Anti-immigrant sentiment in the United States is deeply rooted in its history stemming from psychological, ideological, and social anxieties. As a result, immigrant communities are targeted by xenophobic ideologies. Xenophobia is fundamentally a fear-based reaction, rooted in early developmental anxieties, dissociative defenses, and projections onto those labeled as “strangers.” The visibility of racial minority immigrants in the United States has historically triggered collective anxiety as majority groups externalize fears, losses, and perceived threats onto immigrants

Behind the Myth: Media Manipulation and False Narratives

Media from different eras show how the fears of immigrant ‘invasion’ are recurring issues that are often politically manipulated. These narratives portray immigrants as responsible for unemployment, crime, and threats to cultural integrity, despite a lack of evidence supporting these claims. Public discourse and media rhetoric normalize extreme xenophobic narratives, embedding them into everyday thinking. This normalization occurs when facts about racial minorities are interpreted through what psychoanalyst Fakhry Davids calls a “normal pathological organization,” in which stereotypes and fears are treated as truth and alternative perspectives are defensively rejected

The Human Cost: How the Threat Myth Shapes Policies and Lives

"Immigration Crisis Continues". 2014. Clay Bennett. Chattanooga Times Free Press

The cost of xenophobic rhetoric can fuel internalized racism, fear of deportation, and struggles to balance cultural pride with the pressure to assimilate among immigrant communities. Political figures describing immigrants as “criminals,” “rapists,” and “terrorists,” exemplifies how these political figureheads can exploit these fears for power. These narratives operate as self-protective defenses, enabling people to disavow their own fears, projected aggression, or guilt by assigning them to the “other.” Such language reinforces dissociation, a defense mechanism that allows people to view immigrants as threats rather than fellow human beings.

From Myth to Proof: Fact-Checking Dangerous Lies as a Civic Duty

Reuters.com

Anti-Immigration rhetoric often relies on emotions rather than evidence. In a political climate where fear is weaponized, fact-checking and critical inquiry become moral responsibilities. Truth, grounded in evidence and empathy, is the most effective defense against the lies that sustain racism and xenophobia. With the increased accessibility of technology, social media has become the main source of news for many. Implicit biases and stereotypes become “unleashed in rage” during periods of political upheaval, further distancing people from reality and making evidence-based perspective-taking more difficult.

Healing Through Understanding: A Call for Empathy

"Keep, ancient lands, your storied pomp!" cries she With silent lips. "Give me your tired, your poor, Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free, The wretched refuse of your teeming shore. Send these, the homeless, tempest-tost to me, I lift my lamp beside the golden door!"

Through collective and personal reckoning, society can fight back against false immigrant narratives. The path to healing lies in “witnessing and resisting injustice,” not in denying it. It requires active engagement, self-reflection, and an ongoing willingness to dismantle the internal and external structures that sustain xenophobic thought. In confronting false narratives, we must learn to face the truths behind our defenses: fear of loss, guilt about privilege, and unresolved trauma.
